Real UK Cases

Recordings that changed the outcome

Punjab National Bank v Gosain (EAT, 2014)

[CRISIS] Ms Gosain experienced sex discrimination and sexual harassment at Punjab National Bank International.
[RECORDING] She secretly recorded both her grievance hearing and disciplinary hearing — AND the private panel deliberations after she left the room. The grievance recording (15 min) revealed the managing director had given a direct instruction to dismiss her.
[RESULT] The Employment Appeal Tribunal upheld admissibility of ALL recordings, including the private deliberations. A sexual comment was captured during the adjournment.

Without the recordings? The bank's systematic discrimination would have been covered up entirely.

Phoenix House v Stockman (EAT, 2019)

[CRISIS] Ms Stockman faced unfair dismissal and covertly recorded a meeting with the head of HR.
[RECORDING] The Employment Appeal Tribunal held that covert recordings "will not necessarily amount to a breach of the implied term of trust and confidence."
[RESULT] Won unfair dismissal claim. Compensatory award reduced by only 10% for the covert recording. This case softened the judicial stance on workplace recordings across the UK.

This ruling established that secret recording alone is not automatically gross misconduct.

Medway Council v A — Foster Care Abuse Exposed (Family Court, 2015)

[CRISIS] Parents alleged racist and inappropriate remarks by a local authority foster carer. Their allegations were treated dismissively.
[RECORDING] Parents made covert recordings proving the behaviour.
[RESULT] The Family Court admitted the recordings and relied on them when considering the allegations.

Legal Basis

One-Party Consent: Recording Is Legal in the UK

If you are a party to a conversation, you may record it for personal use without informing the other person. UK law and case law support this position.

RIPA (Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000)

RIPA governs surveillance by public bodies and does not prohibit individuals from recording their own conversations. You are lawfully entitled to record any conversation you are a participant in.

Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR

Voice recordings are personal data. However, recording for personal or household purposes is exempt from most data protection obligations. Use recordings for tribunal or legal proceedings, not for public distribution.

Employment Tribunal Admissibility

Under Tribunal Rule 27, tribunals may admit any evidence considered relevant, including covert recordings. Punjab National Bank v Gosain (2014) and Phoenix House v Stockman (2019) both confirmed admissibility.

Important Warning

Sharing recordings publicly (YouTube, social media) without consent may breach privacy law. Use recordings for tribunal and legal proceedings only. When in doubt, seek advice from a qualified solicitor.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to record a conversation I'm part of in the UK?

Yes. Under RIPA 2000, recording a conversation you are a party to is lawful for personal use. This is known as one-party consent. The law distinguishes this from covert surveillance by a third party. For specific legal advice on your situation, consult a qualified solicitor.

Can covert recordings be used in an employment tribunal?

Yes, in many cases. Employment Tribunal Rule 27 gives the tribunal discretion to admit any relevant evidence. Landmark cases such as Punjab National Bank v Gosain (2014) and Phoenix House v Stockman (2019) have confirmed the admissibility of covert workplace recordings. However, the tribunal may reduce an award if the recording was made in bad faith.

Where are recordings stored?

All recordings are stored on your device only. There are no server uploads, no cloud synchronisation, and the developer cannot access your files under any circumstances.
