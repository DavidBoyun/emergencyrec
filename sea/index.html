Know Your Recording Rights

Recording laws differ by country. Know before you record.

Understanding your local laws can be the difference between protection and prosecution. Always consult a legal professional for your specific situation.

🇵🇭 Philippines

RA 4200 (Anti-Wiretapping Act): ALL-PARTY consent is required for recording private conversations. Recording without consent can result in 6 months to 6 years imprisonment.

  • However: Videos and recordings used to document crimes may be admissible as evidence (People v. Rodriguez, 2024)
  • Recording in public spaces is generally allowed
  • When facing a crime in progress, evidence documentation may be protected
Tip: Know the difference between public and private spaces. When in doubt, consult a lawyer before sharing recordings.

🇮🇩 Indonesia

ITE Law (UU ITE): Electronic recordings are admissible as legal evidence in court proceedings.

  • Warning: Distributing recordings publicly can trigger defamation charges — as seen in the Baiq Nuril case
  • Use recordings for police reports and court proceedings ONLY
  • The 2024 ITE Law amendment added new protections for victims
  • CCTV recordings from your own property are generally admissible
Critical: Never upload recordings to social media before consulting a lawyer. File a police report first.

🇻🇳 Vietnam

Article 95, 2015 Civil Procedure Code: Audio recordings ARE admissible as evidence if collected through legal means.

  • Recording your own conversation is generally permissible
  • Recording others without consent: 1-3 years imprisonment (Penal Code Art. 159)
  • New Personal Data Protection Law effective January 2026 adds additional requirements
  • Workplace recordings may be protected under labor rights provisions
Note: Laws are evolving. The 2026 data protection law may affect recording practices. Stay informed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to record conversations in Southeast Asia?

Recording laws vary by country. In the Philippines, RA 4200 requires all-party consent for private conversations, but evidence of crimes may be admissible. In Indonesia, electronic recordings are admissible evidence, but sharing them publicly can trigger defamation charges. In Vietnam, recording your own conversation is generally allowed, but recording others without consent can lead to penalties. Always check your local laws and consult a legal professional.

Why is this faster than other recording apps?
